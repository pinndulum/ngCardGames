Teaching moments in history...

When learning a new technology, I like to build a solitare project. I've found there are many useful lessons in logic and layout with this strategy. First among these is how interfaces, classes, and objects are organized. Once the frameworks are established, gameplay and useablity can be implemented.

For this iteration, typescript and Angular were my targets. The Tour of Heroes application and tutorial is a wonderful guide and provides many useful lessons.

JM Card Games forms what I believe to be the basis for most table top games. In fact any card game like Uno, Phase 10, and Texas Hold'em; a programmer is limited only be their imagination. Other types of table top games could include blocks, tiles, marbles, chits, or other tokens as well. Games like Dominoes, Mahjong, Connect 4, Othello, etc.

Shuffling a deck of cards

A close look at the source code reveals my solution for shuffling a deck of cards. Rather than iterating each card in a deck and replacing it with another randomly selected card, I believe my soluction is much simpler. First every card is assigned a GUID (globaly unique identifier) that is used to "sort" the deck. The shuffle method includes a parameter that directs how many times this process should occur. This aproximates and simulates how a person would repeat their shuffle multiple times before dealing.

52!

With 52 cards in a deck, the total possible combinations of shuffled cards would equal 52! (52 factorial which is 52 x 51 x 50 x 49...), or 8.06e+67. This is a MASIVE number, so massive that most people have a horrible time understanding the sheer and utter enormity of it. Here is one explanation.

Given 500 Billion galaxies in the universe with 200 Billion solar systems in each galaxy and 1 Billion planets in each solar system and 10 Billion sentient beings on each planet; If it were possible for every one of those beings to shuffle cards in pure random fashion 1 Million times per second since the BEGINNING OF TIME, every possible combination of shuffled cards would still have not been found!

It should be easy to understand that these numbers are purely hypothetical and are in no way accurate, except for the number of solar systems and galaxies in the universe. Obviously, there are NOT 1 billion planets in every solar system. It is also obvious that every planet does not contain 10 billion people.

Somewhat correcting for the errors in our hypothetical senario above; science estimates on average every star has at least 1 planet. To be more than fair, lets assume 2 planets per solar system. For this thought experiment let's just simply ignore that each of those planets can't possibly be inhabited. We will go on believing that there are in fact 10 Billion people on each world. We'll assume that an average human can shuffle a deck of cards in approximately 5 seconds and so can our imaginary life forms.

That would be about 1.58e+50 total card shuffles since the beginning of time. In actuality this represents 1/50 Quintilianth of 52!.
